Decisions, Mistakes & Impact

Standalone architecture for SharePoint Pages

SharePoint Pages has its own existing file format and storage — fundamentally different from Loop/Whiteboard’s native Fluid files. Collaborated with Fluid APIs and Push teams to design and build a standalone architecture that maintained compatibility. Drove it to completion as tech lead.

Engineering quality & guardrails

Invested in high-quality UX (presence indicators, smooth real-time sync), performance optimization to avoid render blocking, and rigorous data loss incident response. Designed the architecture with schema constraints and validation so non-senior engineers could safely contribute features without risking data integrity.

Cross-service observability

Aligned Push and Fluid APIs metrics into our feature dashboard — giving aggregated health visibility across all 4 teams. Made it possible to trace issues end-to-end from client through service layers.

Mistake: late delegation

Held onto too much implementation. At peak, team grew to 6–8 engineers (mix of senior/junior). Once I broke work into swimlanes — seniors on service integration, juniors on client features (presence, exclusive sessions, undo/redo) — things accelerated.

What I'd do differently

Set up clear swimlanes from the start. Focus on architecture and cross-team alignment, let others drive implementation. Would have scaled me faster and grown the team at the same time.

Lesson

Cross-team collaboration requires clear problem statements, proposed alternatives, and efficient communication. Delegation isn’t just about reducing load — it’s about enabling the team to scale faster and giving others growth opportunities.

Lead with the standalone architecture story: Pages uses .aspx files, not .fluid — needed a custom solution across 4 teams. The 3-section model is a concrete technical decision: sections hidden not removed. Cross-service observability shows you thought beyond just your code. Be honest about delegation — swimlanes were the fix. Cross-team lesson: clear problem statements, proposed alternatives, efficient communication.

AI Authoring

Decisions, Mistakes & Impact

Content-first vs. tool-oriented architecture

Initial plan: LLM calls UI tools one-by-one (tool-oriented). I built a POC where the LLM generates the full page structure in one pass (content-first) — like code generation instead of button-clicking. Concrete demo convinced leadership to pivot the entire approach.

Scoping Smart Sections under uncertainty

AI landscape was shifting weekly — team worried about building things that’d be obsolete in months. Partnered with a senior engineer to carve out Smart Sections as a focused, shippable scenario. Two parallel tracks: infrastructure (existing system couldn’t generate web parts) + LLM integration. Delivered prototype, built confidence across leadership.

Managing team anxiety about obsolescence

Team feared their work could become obsolete in months. Made prioritization transparent — laid out ROI for each bet, talked openly about risks, agreed as a group. Shifted from fear-based to evidence-based decision-making.

Mistake: engineering discipline slipped under speed pressure

Moved so fast that documentation and coding discipline slipped. Cutting corners created tech debt that slowed us down later. Learned that LLM integration delivers more impact when built on strong engineering fundamentals — especially in a fast-moving space.

AI Properties — org-wide impact

Designed a schema-driven platform so any team could define LLM-safe representations for their components. Reduced token usage by 30%, improved generation quality, reduced model confusion. Became the foundation that unblocked multiple partner teams.

Growth arc: from CoAuth to AI

CoAuth taught me to delegate and prioritize reliability over speed. AI Authoring taught me to navigate ambiguity and move fast without losing rigor. Applied the swimlane lesson from day one. Together they show I can operate in both multi-year infrastructure and fast-cycle AI spaces.
